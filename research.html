Working Papers

Paying to Run: Withdrawal Fees and Effects on Banking Panic

Banks' chronic vulnerability to panic runs, especially on uninsured deposits, may be mitigated by withdrawal fees that increase with withdrawal demand. To explore this possibility, I introduce and axiomatize a withdrawal-fee policy and characterize the resulting endogenous probability of panic runs. I also develop a microfoundation for the early withdrawal mass through a within-date subgame in which depositors differ in fee tolerance and face transitory liquidity shocks. This allows the policy to affect both liquidity-driven withdrawals and the subsequent signal-driven run equilibrium; this helps to understand how fees affect withdrawal demand. In fact, fees reduce withdrawals, but their effect on run probability is non-monotonic: if the bank offsets the fees’ payoff-reducing effect (an incomplete pass-through condition), any reduction in fragility is neutralized. The model also delivers new trade-offs in the use of fee revenues. I characterize two regimes: one in which collected fees remain invested in the risky project, and another in which they are used to finance partial insurance for waiting depositors when fundamentals are weak. Accordingly, liquidity provision, fragility, and welfare are affected.
